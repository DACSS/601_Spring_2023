Introduction

This data science project aims to explore and analyze a comprehensive dataset of job titles, salaries, employment types, company sizes and locations within the field of data science. With a focus on understanding current trends and patterns, this project seeks to provide insights into the current state of the data science job market. Through analyzing the distribution of job titles, seniority levels, salaries, and employment types, we can gain an understanding of the most common roles, skillsets, and experience required in the field. Additionally, examining the location and size of companies can provide insights into which regions are leading in the data science industry and what size of companies are more likely to hire data scientists. This project aims to provide valuable insights to job seekers, employers, and researchers in the data science field.

Dataset Description

work_year: This column indicates the year in which the salary was paid to the employee. It allows us to analyze trends in salaries over time and compare salaries between different years.

experience_level: This column indicates the experience level of the employee in the job during the year. It allows us to analyze how experience level affects salaries and identify common experience levels for different job titles.

employment_type: This column indicates the type of employment for the role, whether it is Contract, Freelance, Full-Time, or Part-Time. It allows us to analyze the prevalence of different employment types in the data science field.

job_title: This column indicates the role worked in during the year. It allows us to analyze the most common job titles in the data science field and identify trends in job titles over time.

salary: This column indicates the total gross salary amount paid to the employee in the specified currency. It allows us to analyze salary ranges, identify outliers, and compare salaries between different job titles and experience levels.

salary_currency: This column indicates the currency of the salary paid as an ISO 4217 currency code. It allows us to convert salaries to a common currency for analysis and comparison.

salaryinusd: This column indicates the salary in USD. It allows us to compare salaries in a common currency and analyze the impact of currency exchange rates on salaries.

employee_residence: This column indicates the employee’s primary country of residence during the work year as an ISO 3166 country code. It allows us to analyze the geographic distribution of employees and identify common countries of residence for different job titles and experience levels.

remote_ratio: This column indicates the overall amount of work done remotely by the employee during the year. It allows us to analyze the prevalence of remote work in the data science field and identify common remote work ratios for different job titles and experience levels.

company_location: This column indicates the country of the employer’s main office or contracting branch. It allows us to analyze the geographic distribution of companies and identify common countries where data science jobs are located.

company_size: This column indicates the median number of people that worked for the company during the year. It allows us to analyze the size distribution of companies and identify common company sizes for different job titles and experience levels.

check <- function(data) {
  l <- list()
  columns <- names(data)
  for (col in columns) {
    instances <- sum(!is.na(data[[col]]))
    dtypes <- class(data[[col]])
    unique <- length(unique(data[[col]]))
    sum_null <- sum(is.na(data[[col]]))
    duplicates <- sum(duplicated(data))
    l[[length(l) + 1]] <- c(col, dtypes, instances, unique, sum_null, duplicates)
  }
  data_check <- as.data.frame(do.call(rbind, l))
  names(data_check) <- c("column", "dtype", "instances", "unique", "sum_null", "duplicates")

}

mode <- function(x){
  ta <- table(x)
  tam <- max(ta)
  if(all(ta==tam))

  else
    if(is.numeric(x))
      mod <- as.numeric(names(ta)[ta==tam])
  else
    mod <- names(ta)[ta==tam]

work_year: This column indicates the year in which the salary was paid to the employee. It allows us to analyze trends in salaries over time and compare salaries between different years.

experience_level: This column indicates the experience level of the employee in the job during the year. It allows us to analyze how experience level affects salaries and identify common experience levels for different job titles.

employment_type: This column indicates the type of employment for the role, whether it is Contract, Freelance, Full-Time, or Part-Time. It allows us to analyze the prevalence of different employment types in the data science field.

salary: This column indicates the total gross salary amount paid to the employee in the specified currency. It allows us to analyze salary ranges, identify outliers, and compare salaries between different job titles and experience levels.

# Conclusion

Job Titles: Salaries in the data science field vary based on job titles. Executive-level positions tend to have higher salaries compared to entry-level, mid-level, and senior-level positions.

Seniority Levels: Salaries increase with increasing seniority levels. Executive-level salaries are higher than those of entry-level, mid-level, and senior-level positions, while senior-level salaries are higher than those of entry-level and mid-level positions.

Employment Types: Full-time employment is the most common form of employment in the data science field and typically comes with higher salaries compared to other employment types such as contract, freelance, and part-time.

Company Sizes: Medium-sized companies in the data science field tend to pay higher salaries compared to large-sized companies. This suggests that the size of the company can impact the salary levels offered to data scientists.

Geographic Location: The United States has the highest concentration of data science jobs. Salaries may vary depending on the location, with regions with a higher demand for data scientists offering higher salaries.

Salary Trends: Salaries in the data science domain have been increasing year on year, with a significant rise in 2022. This indicates that there is a positive trend in salary growth, and it is expected to continue in 2023.

Work Arrangements: A significant portion of individuals in the data science field have some level of flexibility in their work arrangements. This suggests that work flexibility may not significantly impact salary levels in the data science job market.

Remote Work: Salaries of remote employees in the data science field are comparable to those of on-site employees. This indicates that the option to work remotely does not have a significant impact on salary levels in the data science job market.

Overall, these findings suggest that the Data Science field is a growing and lucrative industry, with potential for career growth and financial success. However, factors such as employment type, company size, and location may impact an individual's earning potential.
